### XLP â€” The Sector Standard

XLP is the **most liquid and widely traded** consumer staples ETF. It tracks the S&P 500 Consumer Staples sector, holding only large-cap stocks.

**Top holdings (approximate):**
- Walmart (WMT): 10-12%
- Costco (COST): 9-10%
- Procter & Gamble (PG): 8-9%
- Coca-Cola (KO): 6%
- Philip Morris (PM): 5-6%
- PepsiCo (PEP): 5%

**Sector breakdown:**
- Consumer Staples Distribution & Retail: ~33%
- Beverages: ~20%
- Food Products: ~17%
- Household Products: ~17%
- Tobacco: ~10%
- Personal Care Products: ~4%

**When to use XLP:**
- You want the most liquid staples exposure
- You're using options (best liquidity)
- You want S&P 500-only exposure
- You're making tactical defensive rotations

**Concentration note:** XLP is more diversified than XLY. Top holdings are 10-12% vs. 20%+ for discretionary. Less single-stock risk.

### VDC â€” The Broader Approach

VDC tracks a broader index (MSCI US Investable Market Consumer Staples) with about **100 holdings** compared to XLP's 40.

**Key differences from XLP:**
- **More holdings**: ~100 vs ~40
- **Small/mid-cap exposure**: Includes stocks below S&P 500
- **Slightly higher expense**: 0.10% vs 0.08%
- **Longer track record**: Inception 2004

**When to use VDC:**
- You want broader diversification within staples
- You believe mid-cap staples will participate
- You prefer Vanguard's platform
- Long-term holding

### FSTA â€” The Cost-Efficient Alternative

FSTA offers **tied-for-lowest expense ratio** at 0.08% while tracking MSCI's Consumer Staples index.

**Key differences:**
- **Cheapest option**: Tied with XLP at 0.08%
- **Broader holdings**: ~100 stocks (more than XLP)
- **MSCI index**: Slightly different composition than S&P

**When to use FSTA:**
- Cost is your primary concern
- You want broader exposure similar to VDC
- Long-term holding

## Why Staples Are Defensive

Consumer staples have historically provided:

| Characteristic | Why It Matters |
|----------------|----------------|
| **Lower beta** | Less volatile than the market (beta ~0.6-0.7) |
| **Steady earnings** | Demand is consistent regardless of economy |
| **Dividend yield** | Higher yield than growth sectors (~2.5-3%) |
| **Recession resistance** | Essential spending continues during downturns |
| **Inflation hedge** | Pricing power on branded products |

### The Trade-Off

Staples typically **underperform during bull markets**. When risk appetite is high, money flows to growth and cyclicals. Staples get left behind.

| Market Environment | Staples Behavior |
|--------------------|------------------|
| Bull market, risk-on | Underperform (laggard) |
| Bear market, risk-off | Outperform (safe haven) |
| High volatility | Relative stability |
| Rising rates | Mixed (higher yields compete) |
| Recession | Outperform significantly |

|--------|------------------|---------------------|

### When RSPS Makes Sense

- You want broader staples exposure without retail dominance
- You believe smaller staples names will catch up
- You're pairing with RSPD for equal-weight consumer ratio analysis
- You want more exposure to food/beverage producers vs. retailers

### The Walmart/Costco Question

Walmart and Costco together represent ~20% of XLP. These are **retailers**, not product manufacturers:

- **Walmart**: Value-focused, trade-down beneficiary during recessions
- **Costco**: Membership model, higher-income customer base

Their inclusion makes XLP somewhat different from "pure" staples exposure. They behave more like hybrid retail/staples.

XLP tends to underperform during:

- Strong growth-led advances
