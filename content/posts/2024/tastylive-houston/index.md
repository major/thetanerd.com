## Liz

[Liz](https://www.tastylive.com/talent/liz-dierking) kicked things off by giving her top eight mindset adjustments for becoming a better trader.
She said that joining an improvisational comedy group really helped her think in the moment and make quick decisions on her feet.
As for books, she said that ["Blink" by Malcolm Gladwell](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/40102.Blink) was a game changer for her.

There were three things from her talk that really got me thinking:

1. **Make statements, not questions.**
   Speak definitively about things and seek others who will argue with you on a topic.
   Avoid ending sentences like a question and make them more like statements.
2. **Be all in.**
   She gave an example of being in a parade versus watching it.
   If you spend all your time watching the market without getting in, your life is filled with "should have" and "could have" rather than "I did", even if the result wasn't great.
   In the end, it's better to be all in and fail rather than do something halfway and think about what might have been.
3. **Yes, and...**
   Accept what you're given and add to it.
   She made a point that trading is 2% what happens to you and 98% how you react to it.
   Trading it more about _will_ than _intellect_.

## Anton

[Anton](https://www.tastylive.com/talent/anton-kulikov) kicked off a talk about market efficiency and avoiding situations where you believe the market is rigged against you.
He offered some good arguments against that, especially because you can take the opposite trade to any trade and pay the same cost.

This was confusing at first, but he compared opening spreads to playing at a roulette table.
It began to make a lot more sense when you could see probability, risk, and reward shifting on the roulette table and in financial markets.

He extended this to the question of when two people want a slice of cake and the slice needs to be cut evenly.
Normally, one person cuts and the other person chooses which slice they take.
This really encourages the first person to cut a fair slice.
The market works the same way.

## Jenny

[Jenny](https://www.tastylive.com/talent/jenny-andrews) argued for [Jade Lizard](https://www.tastylive.com/concepts-strategies/jade-lizard) trades as the best ones available.
She got her start with naked puts and gradually moved into short strangles.

Jade Lizards offer more protection for the short call and a higher return on risked capital.

## Julia

[Julia](https://www.tastylive.com/talent/julia-spina) talked about her love for [Iron Condors](https://www.tastylive.com/concepts-strategies/iron-condor).
That's a neutral strategy that is a lot like a short strangle but you get to choose how much to risk on the edges.

They're easy to do in cash accounts and they use a lot less buying power.

## Dr. Jim

[Dr. Jim](https://www.tastylive.com/talent/jim-schultz) offered a very entertaining talk about my favorite trade: [short puts](https://www.tastylive.com/concepts-strategies/naked-options#naked-short-put).

It's a simple strategy with one leg and you get the best of the options greeks:

* Positive theta
* Negative vega
* Positive delta

You make money on these if price goes up, goes sideways, or goes down a little.

## Tom

[Tom](https://www.tastylive.com/talent/tom-sosnoff) needed no introduction. 😜

He started off with some funny stories from the ThinkOrSwim days and about Tony's directional challenges, but he went into a really casual discussion about artificial intelligence (AI) and trading.
Tastytrade has worked on machine learning for years to see if there's a way that AI can help with:

* Picking stocks
* Picking strategies
* Picking where the market is going tomorrow

After a lot of work, they repeatedly found that AI did no better than a coin flip on picking stocks, strategies, or directions of markets.
In addition, quantifying risk is nearly impossible to do with a machine.

AI is trained on what happened in the past.
So are we.
That's why we struggle to predict what the market might do tomorrow even when we know the odds of how it acted in the past.
Predicting human behavior is incredibly challenging.

Tom felt that AI could help a lot with trader education.
For example, traders might be able to ask AI software about a particular type of trade and how it has worked in the past.
It would be much faster for a computer to dig up the information and report it.

After talking about AI, he covered [ratio spreads](https://www.tastylive.com/concepts-strategies/ratio-spread).
He said he enjoys using them for earnings trades.

## Other traders

I spoke with other traders before and after the event.
Most said they were profitable in their trades and it seemed like most of them were doing some kind of spread trading (short/long vertical spreads).
Iron condors also seemed fairly popular.
A smaller amount were trading short puts primarily and almost nobody I spoke to was doing short strangles.

We talked about some of the tools we use to help with trading and I was shocked to hear how many traders were following other traders to make trades.
Sure, tastylive does offer a live feed of the trades from their on-air personalities, but many people I spoke to used one or more paid services to get trade alerts via SMS or Telegram.

When they asked me what I use, I went through my process:

1. Set rules in [my screener on barchart.com](https://www.barchart.com/options/naked-puts).
2. Look at the trades in the screener and examine the underlying's charts.
3. Make trades from that list if the chart shows a recognizable pattern.

This allows me to be mechanical and efficient when making trades.
I only see trades that follow my rules and I'm not tempted to trade anything else.
This keeps me out of trouble.
_Mostly._ 😉
