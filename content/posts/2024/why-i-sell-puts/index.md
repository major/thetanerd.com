When I talk to people about investing and trading, I'm often asked the same set of questions:

> Why sell puts?
> That seems complicated.
> Shouldn't you just buy and hold stocks?

There are plenty of reasons for and against trading options versus stocks, but one of the greatest things about financial markets is that you can approach them with your preferred strategy and timelines.
Some trades in the market last fractions of a second, some last years, and all of these trades are made with different goals in mind.

{{< disclaimer >}}

Let's get started!

## Picking levels, not targets

One of the most challenging parts of the market is knowing when to get into a trade and when to get out.
Traders use all kinds of data to make entry and exit decisions, but picking bottoms and tops on a chart is _extremely difficult_.

Even the best trading firms on the planet, with their highly educated traders and incredible technology, struggle to pick when a market is at it's lowest or at the top.

Selling puts allows me to pick a level on a chart and make a trade.
For example, if I think a particular level offers a better chance of finding buyers (a support level), I can sell a put there.
The stock can trade higher, trade sideways, or come down a little bit to the level I've chosen.
**In all three of those scenarios, I stand to make a profit.**

## Simple, mechanical exits

I stumbled upon the [Theta Gang Podcast](https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/theta-gang/id1474557787) back in 2020 and learned a ton about selling options.
Much of it made little sense at the beginning, but then the concepts started coming together.

Joonie, the person behind the podcast and the [Theta Gang](https://thetagang.com/) website, talked about entering a closing order _immediately after selling an option._
His argument was that setting a consistent profit target, such as a 50% gain, takes emotion out of the trade and makes the trade much more mechanical and repeatable.

In addition, it's a great way to secure profits when you're not available for active trading.
Nothing's better than getting a random ding from your trading app in the middle of the day letting you know you've secured a profit from a trade. 😉

Markets move fast, and these fast moves cause option prices to fluctuate wildly.
Setting a 50% profit trigger right after selling a put can allow you to capture profits during these swings and get some risk off the table earlier.
There's value in that as well.
Every trade comes with risk and removing risk from your account while capturing profits is a double win.

## Diversification

Selling puts allows me to move in and out of trades rather frequently.
This means I can move between stocks and ETFs in different sectors on a regular basis.
I'm not tying a large chunk of my account to holding shares from a particular company.

Automatic profit triggers _(from the last section)_ and lower capital requirements _(see the next section)_ contribute to this, too.
If semiconductors have a good day and some of my short puts close, I might move into energy the next day when semiconductors struggle.
It allows me to better move with the flow of the market.

## Lower capital requirements

Buying stock takes up a lot of capital, even if your account has [margin](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/margin.asp).
Without margin, buying 100 shares of a $100 stock takes up $10,000 in capital in your account.
For some stocks, you can get 30-40% margin from your brokerage and that only takes up $3,000-$4,000 worth of capital.

{{< alert >}}
**Margin must be used very carefully.**
Although it can be used for leverage to increase profits, it's a double edged sword.
Over leveraging an account with margin can lead to serious shortfalls during market drops.
Be careful with margin accounts and always know how much buying power you are using at all times.
**You can be left in a situation where you actually owe your brokerage more money than you have.**
{{< /alert >}}

Selling puts use even less margin than holding stocks in a margin account.
[TastyTrade](https://support.tastytrade.com/support/s/solutions/articles/43000435177) has an example where selling a put on a $47.50 stock only consumes about $750 of margin.
Buying and holding 100 shares of a $47.50 stock would be $4,750 without margin or possibly $1,500-$2,000 with margin.

## Everything has risks

Any trade you make in the market has risk and [knowing your max loss](/p/know-your-max-loss/) for any trade is crucial.
When you buy stock and the stock price drops, you begin losing money immediately.

When you sell a put and the stock price drops, the value of your put drops, too.
However, you still have some breathing room until the stock price breaches your strike and approaches your break even point.

If you sell a put on a $100 stock and collect $1.00 in premium (that's $100 collected since it's $1.00 x 100 shares), your break even is $99.
Any stock price above $99 means you're still in a profit zone.

Let's say you sold that $100 put on a stock trading at $110.

If you bought 100 shares at $110 and the price falls to $100, you just lost $1,000[^unrealized].

Your put would certainly be in the red, but the price is still $1 over your break even point.
As long as it stays above $99, you're still in a profit zone.
This "cushion" is another reason why I enjoy selling puts.

## Summary

Selling puts comes with risks but it also provides some breathing room for trades where you benefit from stocks trading up, sideways, or down a little.
It's easier on the capital in your account, provided that you understand the margin risks.

Traders who enjoy making trades frequently and moving between various stocks and ETFs might enjoy the mechanical nature of put selling.
I'll detail more on my mechanical process in a future post. 😉

[^unrealized]: This loss is only "on paper" and not fully realized yet.
