Business Development Companies (BDCs) are publicly traded companies that lend to middle-market businesses ‚Äî companies too small for investment banks but too large for traditional bank loans. BDC ETFs provide access to this private credit market with yields that can exceed 10%.

## Why BDCs matter

- **Extreme yields**: 9-12%+ dividend yields, among the highest in public markets
- **Floating rate loans**: Most BDC loans are floating rate, benefiting from higher rates
- **Private credit exposure**: Access to middle-market lending typically reserved for institutions
- **Monthly or quarterly income**: Regular distributions for income-focused investors
- **Economic sensitivity**: BDC performance reflects small/mid-cap business health

## How BDCs work

BDCs are required by law to:
- Distribute at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders (like REITs)
- Invest at least 70% of assets in qualifying private US companies
- Maintain asset coverage ratios (limits on leverage)

This structure creates **high yields** but also **high payout ratios** ‚Äî BDCs retain little earnings for growth.

| BDC Characteristic | Implication |
|-------------------|-------------|
| **90%+ distribution** | High yields, but little cushion if loans go bad |
| **Floating rate loans** | Rising rates = higher income (until borrowers default) |
| **Middle-market focus** | Exposed to small business cycle |
| **Leverage limits** | Can't overlever, but still use significant debt |

## The BDC risk profile

{{< callout type="warning" >}}
**This is not a bond substitute**: BDCs have equity-like volatility with high sensitivity to credit cycles and economic downturns. The 10%+ yield comes with 10%+ potential drawdowns.
{{< /callout >}}

### What can go wrong

| Risk | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Credit losses** | If borrowers default, BDC NAV drops and dividends get cut |
| **Rate reversal** | Floating rates help now, but rapidly falling rates = lower income |
| **Economic recession** | Middle-market companies fail first ‚Äî BDC portfolios suffer |
| **NAV erosion** | BDCs often trade at premiums/discounts to NAV; in stress, discounts widen |
| **Dividend cuts** | When credit losses mount, dividends get slashed |

### Historical behavior

| Environment | BDC Behavior |
|-------------|--------------|
| **Strong economy, rising rates** | Excellent ‚Äî higher income, low defaults |
| **Stable economy** | Good ‚Äî steady income |
| **Slowing economy** | Caution ‚Äî credit concerns emerge |
| **Recession** | Poor ‚Äî defaults spike, dividends cut, prices crash |
| **Recovery** | Strong bounce ‚Äî survivors benefit |

## BDCs vs. alternatives

| Goal | BDCs | Alternative | Tradeoff |
|------|------|-------------|----------|
| High yield | 10%+ | HYG (5-6%) | More yield, more risk |
| Floating rate | Yes | BKLN, SRLN | BDCs riskier, higher yield |
| Private credit | Yes | Interval funds | BDCs liquid but volatile |
| Diversified income | Concentrated | Preferreds, MLPs | BDCs = middle-market credit only |

## When to consider BDCs

‚úÖ **BDCs may work when**:
- Economy is stable or expanding
- Interest rates are elevated (floating rate benefit)
- You can tolerate equity-like volatility
- You're focused on current income over total return
- You understand credit cycle dynamics

‚ùå **BDCs are risky when**:
- Recession approaching (credit losses ahead)
- Rates are falling rapidly (income declines)
- You need principal stability
- You're treating them as "bond alternatives"

**The bottom line**: BDCs offer genuine 10%+ yields, but you're taking real credit risk on middle-market loans. They're not bonds ‚Äî they behave like small-cap financials in stress. Use them for income in good times, but size positions appropriately and understand that dividends can be cut and prices can drop 20%+ in recessions.
