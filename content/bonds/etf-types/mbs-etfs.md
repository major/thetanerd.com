Mortgage-backed securities (MBS) are bonds backed by pools of home mortgages. When homeowners make mortgage payments, those cash flows pass through to MBS holders. This creates a unique bond type with **housing market exposure** and **prepayment risk**.

## Why MBS matter

MBS occupy a unique space in fixed income:

- **Housing market exposure**: MBS performance reflects housing health
- **Prepayment risk**: When rates fall, homeowners refinance, shortening duration
- **Fed holdings**: The Fed owns trillions in MBS ‚Äî policy directly affects this market
- **Spread to treasuries**: MBS spreads reflect housing and credit conditions
- **Government backing**: Agency MBS are effectively government-guaranteed

## The prepayment problem

MBS have **negative convexity** ‚Äî they behave asymmetrically to rate changes:

| Rate Environment | What Happens | MBS Impact |
|------------------|--------------|------------|
| Rates fall sharply | Homeowners refinance | Prepayments accelerate, you get principal back early (at the worst time) |
| Rates rise sharply | No refinancing | Duration extends, you're stuck longer |

This prepayment risk is why MBS yields are higher than comparable treasuries. You're being compensated for the uncertainty.

### How prepayment affects returns

Think of it this way:

- **Rates fall**: You want to hold a high-yielding bond longer, but homeowners refinance and pay you back. Now you must reinvest at lower rates.
- **Rates rise**: You'd prefer to sell and buy a higher-yielding bond, but no one is refinancing. Your capital is locked up in a lower-yielding asset.

This asymmetry is the price of the yield premium MBS offer.

## MBS as a Fed policy indicator

The Fed's MBS holdings and purchase/sales plans directly affect this market:

| Fed Action | MBS Impact | Spread Effect |
|------------|------------|---------------|
| Buying MBS | Supportive of housing | Spreads tighten to treasuries |
| Selling/runoff | Less support | Spreads may widen |
| Balance sheet expansion | Demand for MBS rises | Positive for prices |
| Quantitative tightening | Demand falls | Negative for prices |

Watch Fed balance sheet announcements for MBS policy changes. The Fed owns trillions in agency MBS, making it the dominant holder.

## How MBS move in different regimes

| Scenario | MBS Behavior | Why |
|----------|--------------|-----|
| Fed cutting rates | Rally, but prepayment risk rises | Homeowners refinance |
| Fed hiking rates | Fall, duration extends | No refinancing, stuck longer |
| Recession fear | Slight spread widening | Credit concerns (even though agency-backed) |
| Housing market stress | Spreads widen | Prepayment uncertainty increases |
| Stable rates | Steady income, low volatility | Predictable cash flows |
